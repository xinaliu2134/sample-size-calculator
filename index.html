Single Proportion

Estimate required sample size to detect a proportion with specified precision or compare against a known value.

Parameters
Your best estimate of the outcome proportion in the population.
Zα/2 = 1.960
Zβ = 0.842
Half-width of the confidence interval (e.g., 0.05 = ±5%).

Statistical Theory

For precision-based estimation, the formula derives from the normal approximation to the binomial distribution. The margin of error d represents half the width of the confidence interval. For hypothesis testing, the formula accounts for both Type I (α) and Type II (β) error rates.

Precision: n = Z²α/2 × p(1-p) / d²
Hypothesis: n = [Zα/2√p̄(1-p̄) + Zβ√p(1-p)]² / (p-p₀)²

Single Mean

Estimate required sample size to estimate a mean with specified precision, or to test a mean against a reference value.

Parameters
From pilot study or literature.
Margin of error (precision) or minimum detectable difference.

Statistical Theory

For continuous outcomes, sample size depends on the standard deviation and desired precision. The coefficient of variation (CV = σ/μ) can help estimate σ when direct data are unavailable. For hypothesis testing, the detectable difference δ represents the minimum clinically or epidemiologically meaningful effect.

Precision: n = Z²α/2 × σ² / d²
Hypothesis: n = (Zα/2 + Zβ)² × σ² / δ²

Two Independent Proportions

Compare two groups (e.g., treatment vs. control) on a binary outcome. Commonly used in RCTs and cohort studies.

Parameters
Exposed / Treatment group
Unexposed / Control group
Zα/2 = 1.960
Zβ = 0.842
1 = equal groups; 2 = 2× more in group 1

Statistical Theory

The two-proportion z-test uses the pooled proportion under H₀ and separate proportions under H₁. The formula yields per-group sample sizes; total N is the sum. The allocation ratio allows unequal group sizes when one group is harder to recruit.

Formula: n = [Zα/2√(2p̄q̄) + Zβ√(p₁q₁ + p₂q₂)]² / (p₁-p₂)²

k Independent Proportions (χ² Test)

Compare proportions across 3 or more independent groups. Uses chi-square test with Cohen's w effect size. Suitable for multi-arm trials, dose-response studies, and multi-region comparisons.

Parameters
Enter the anticipated proportion (0–1) for each group
Group 1

Statistical Theory

Sample size is derived from the non-central chi-square distribution. Cohen's w measures the overall effect across all groups: w = √[Σ(pᵢ − p̄)² / p̄]. The overall test (df = k−1) detects any difference among groups. When pairwise comparisons matter, apply Bonferroni correction: α* = α / C(k,2).

Formula: find n per group such that P(χ²(df, λ) > χ²crit) ≥ power, where λ = n·k·w²

Cohort Study (Relative Risk)

Prospective or retrospective cohort study comparing incidence rates between exposed and unexposed groups.

Parameters
e.g., 2 = 2 unexposed per exposed

Epidemiological Basis

In cohort studies, the Relative Risk (RR = p₁/p₀) is the primary measure of association. The Kelsey formula or Schlesselman formula for cohort studies uses the average proportion p̄ = (p₁ + cp₀)/(1+c) where c is the allocation ratio.

Attributable Risk: AR = p₁ - p₀
Population AR%: PAR% = (RR-1)/RR × prevalence × 100

Case-Control Study (Odds Ratio)

Retrospective study design comparing exposure history between cases and controls. Especially efficient for rare diseases.

Parameters
Proportion exposed among controls
OR > 1 = positive association
More controls increases power without extra cases

Epidemiological Basis

The Kelsey formula for case-control studies uses the OR to derive the exposure probability in cases (p₁). When the disease is rare, OR ≈ RR. Increasing the controls-to-cases ratio beyond 4:1 yields diminishing returns in power.

p₁ from OR: p₁ = OR × p₀ / (1 - p₀ + OR × p₀)
Max efficiency: c = 4 controls per case

RCT — Parallel Group Design

The standard two-arm randomized controlled trial. Supports binary, continuous, and non-inferiority/equivalence trial configurations.

Parameters
Maximum acceptable difference to declare non-inferiority or equivalence.
1 = equal; 2 = 2 treatment per 1 control

RCT Design Notes

Superiority trials test whether treatment is better than control. Non-inferiority trials test whether treatment is not worse than control by more than margin Δ (one-sided α). Equivalence trials test whether treatment and control differ by less than Δ in either direction (two-sided α, doubled sample size).

Superiority (binary): n = [Zα/2√(2p̄q̄) + Zβ√(p₁q₁+p₀q₀)]² / (p₁-p₀)²
Non-inferiority: Use one-sided Zα; replace (p₁-p₀) with (p₁-p₀-Δ)
Equivalence: Use two-sided; n × 2

RCT — Crossover Design (2×2)

Each participant receives both treatments in sequence (A→B or B→A). More efficient than parallel design; requires stable chronic conditions and washout period.

Parameters
σ_d = σ × √(2(1-ρ)), where ρ = correlation between periods
Correlation of measurements within the same subject across periods. Higher ρ = greater efficiency gain.

Crossover Design Theory

In a 2×2 crossover trial, each subject serves as their own control, removing between-subject variability. The effective SD is the within-subject SD of differences: σ_d = σ × √(2(1-ρ)). When ρ is high (e.g., 0.7+), crossover requires far fewer subjects than a parallel trial.

Continuous: n = (Zα/2 + Zβ)² × 2σ²(1-ρ) / δ²
Efficiency vs parallel: RE = 1 / (2(1-ρ)) — crossover needs ~(1-ρ) × parallel n

RCT — Cluster Randomised Trial (CRT)

Randomization by cluster (e.g., clinics, schools, villages) rather than individuals. Requires inflation by the Design Effect (DEFF) due to within-cluster correlation (ICC).

Parameters
Typical range: 0.01–0.10 for health outcomes; higher for behavioral outcomes.
Number of individuals per cluster (e.g., per clinic).

Cluster RCT Theory

In cluster RCTs, individuals within the same cluster share common exposures, making their outcomes correlated. This violates the independence assumption of standard formulas. The Design Effect (DEFF) inflates the required sample size to account for this correlation.

DEFF = 1 + (m-1) × ICC
Adjusted n = n_individual × DEFF
Clusters per arm = ⌈n_adjusted / (m × arms)⌉

Survival Analysis: Time-to-Event

Log-rank test for comparing time-to-event outcomes between two groups. Used in clinical trials and longitudinal cohort studies.

Parameters
E.g., 0.70 = 70% survive
Control / reference group

Statistical Theory

The Schoenfeld formula calculates the required number of events (deaths/failures) for the log-rank test. Subject numbers are then back-calculated from expected event probabilities. The hazard ratio (HR) is derived from the survival functions assuming exponential distributions.

Events needed: E = 4(Zα/2 + Zβ)² / ln(HR)²
HR from survival: HR = ln(S₁)/ln(S₀)

General Guidance on Assumptions

α = 0.05 and 1-β = 0.80 are standard defaults. In high-stakes situations (drug approval, safety studies), consider α = 0.01 and power ≥ 0.90.

Always inflate the sample size by the expected attrition rate: n_final = n_base / (1 - attrition%). For longitudinal studies, attrition of 15–30% is common.

For cluster-randomized studies, multiply by the design effect (DEFF) = 1 + (m-1) × ICC, where m = cluster size and ICC = intraclass correlation coefficient.

These calculators use asymptotic (large-sample) normal approximations. For very small proportions or rare events, exact methods (Binomial, Fisher exact) may be more appropriate.

Foundational Textbooks
Epidemiology

Modern Epidemiology, 3rd ed.

Rothman KJ, Greenland S, Lash TL (eds.)

Lippincott Williams & Wilkins, Philadelphia, 2008

The definitive reference for epidemiological study design, confounding, bias, and effect measure selection. Chapters 14–16 cover sample size and power extensively for cohort, case-control, and cross-sectional designs.

Epidemiology

Epidemiologic Research: Principles and Quantitative Methods

Kelsey JL, Whittemore AS, Evans AS, Thompson WD

Van Nostrand Reinhold, New York, 1996

Source of the widely-used Kelsey formula for cohort and case-control sample size calculations. Provides detailed derivations for ratio-unequal designs and finite-population corrections.

Biostatistics

Statistical Methods for Rates and Proportions, 3rd ed.

Fleiss JL, Levin B, Paik MC

John Wiley & Sons, Hoboken NJ, 2003

The standard reference for sample size formulas involving proportions. Chapters 3–4 derive the two-proportion z-test formula; Chapter 14 covers matched designs. Equations implemented directly in this calculator's Two Proportions and Case-Control modules.

Biostatistics

Sample Size Calculations in Clinical Research, 3rd ed.

Chow S-C, Shao J, Wang H, Lokhnygina Y

CRC Press / Taylor & Francis, Boca Raton FL, 2018

Comprehensive coverage of sample size methodology for parallel, crossover, and cluster RCTs. Includes non-inferiority and equivalence trial frameworks. Directly referenced for the RCT modules in this calculator.

Survival

Sample size considerations for the log-rank statistic in clinical trials

Schoenfeld D

Biometrics, 39(2): 499–503, 1983

Original derivation of the events-based sample size formula for the log-rank test (Schoenfeld formula). The survival module in this calculator implements equation (2) from this paper: E = 4(Zα/2+Zβ)² / [ln(HR)]².

RCT / Design

Design and Analysis of Clinical Trials: Concepts and Methodologies, 3rd ed.

Chow S-C, Liu J-P

John Wiley & Sons, Hoboken NJ, 2014

Covers superiority, non-inferiority, and equivalence margins; crossover carryover corrections; and adaptive design considerations. Reference for the crossover intrasubject correlation (ρ) adjustment used in this calculator.

RCT / Design

Statistical Issues in Drug Development, 2nd ed.

Senn S

John Wiley & Sons, Chichester, 2007

Authoritative treatment of crossover trials, period effects, and variance components. Chapter 8 provides the paired-difference formulation for crossover sample size, including the role of σwithin and the correlation coefficient ρ.

Cluster RCT

Donner A, Klar N

Arnold / Hodder Headline, London, 2000

Definitive reference for intraclass correlation (ICC) and design effect (DEFF = 1 + (m−1)×ICC) in cluster-randomized trials. Chapter 3 derives the inflation formula implemented in the Cluster RCT module.
